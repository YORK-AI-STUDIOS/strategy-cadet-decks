Part 10 · Extended Analysis · Customer Problem Framework, Seven Named Problems (Section 10)

Seven distinct named customer problems spanning the modular B2B and RV consumer worlds. Each is framed in the language the buyer would use. The four B2B problems are commercially urgent; the three RV problems are emotionally loaded. Probable

B2B problems (modular, 4 of 7)
Problem 1 of 7
Schedule Slippage as Career Risk, Not Commercial Inconvenience
Inside Tier 1 contractors and state public-works departments, accommodation delivery slippage is now a career-defining event. A project director who fails to stand up a 1,200-bed village inside the FID-to-first-pour window has personally cost an iron ore expansion months of production.
"A Pilbara expansion approved at FID in February needs 1,200 beds operational by the following dry season, any vendor who cannot commit in writing is non-responsive."
Audience 1: Resources and Defence
Problem 2 of 7
Probity-Defensibility as the Silent Disqualifier
Public-sector modular buyers live inside AusTender, panel arrangements and ministerial briefings. They cannot specify a vendor who lacks the paper trail to survive an Auditor-General review, even if the product is superior. The procurement officer who picks the wrong vendor wears that decision for five years of their career.
"A state housing authority needs 800 dwellings in 18 months, the decisive factor between two vendors is which one already sits on the Local Buy panel."
Audience 2: Public-Sector Modular
Problem 3 of 7
Retention Erosion at AUD 200,000-Plus Salary Bands
Australian resources operators pay record salaries to keep experienced workers on roster. Village amenity is now a retention lever. A workforce costing AUD 500,000 per head per year to replace is worth insuring through premium accommodation.
"A coal operator hit 38% rotation turnover, exit interviews consistently named the camp, and the next RFP added a wellbeing-amenity weighting."
Audience 1: Resources and Defence
Problem 4 of 7
Psychosocial-Safety Regulatory Drift Outpacing Vendor Product Roadmaps
AU WHS regulation now imposes explicit psychosocial-safety duty-of-care on operators. Workforce accommodation procurers must specify rooms and layouts that defend against fatigue, isolation and acoustic stress, but most modular vendor product literature still leads with "modern, comfortable rooms" rather than evidenced wellbeing performance.
"An LNG operator after a mental-health incident needs a camp where every room passes an acoustic and privacy audit, with an evidence pack the regulator will accept."
Audience 1: Resources and Defence
RV / consumer problems (3 of 7)
Problem 5 of 7
The Bifurcating Caravan Market Squeezing the Middle
The Australian caravan category is splitting into a premium-domestic tier (Jayco at 37% of Australian-made share) and a value-import tier (Chinese-built entry product). Coromal and Windsor sit in the squeezed middle, with neither the scale of Jayco nor the price of imports. "Australian-made" alone has become table stakes rather than a differentiator.
"A 62-year-old Perth pre-retiree comparing a AUD 78,000 Coromal against a AUD 62,000 Chinese-built equivalent wants a credible reason to spend the extra AUD 16,000."
Audience 3: Grey Nomad Caravan Buyers
Problem 6 of 7
Off-Grid Capability Mismatched to Actual Touring Patterns
Grey nomad buyers are increasingly off-grid focused but most marketing leads with maximum-spec lithium-solar systems that are over-engineered for typical touring use. The deeper problem is that buyers cannot easily benchmark "what level of off-grid is right for the way we actually want to travel." The category competes on spec-sheet headlines instead of trip-typology guidance.
"A recently-retired couple planning three two-month trips per year between caravan parks does not need a 600Ah lithium system, they need a framework matching power and water to their actual trip archetypes."
Audience 3: Grey Nomad Caravan Buyers
Problem 7 of 7
The Post-Purchase Fade in Dealer Service Network Reliability
The most consistent RV customer complaint is not the van itself, it is what happens after the purchase: warranty claims, dealer responsiveness on the road and mid-trip service availability in regional Australia. The forums that grey nomads actually read socialise warranty horror stories and those stories shape brand consideration far more than gloss campaigns.
"A Big-Lap couple needs a chassis weld repair in Mount Isa, the brand whose dealer can get them moving inside 72 hours wins the next-van decision and the recommendation to three forum readers."
Audience 3: Grey Nomad Caravan Buyers
